Internal Programming

Beyond external cues, seeds have internal controls that dictate when germination occurs. These include dormancy mechanisms and a delicate hormonal balance. Click the cards to learn more.

üå± Seed Dormancy

A survival strategy to prevent germination until conditions are truly optimal, not just suitable.

Physiological: Most common type, caused by internal chemical inhibitors. Broken by chilling (stratification).

Physical: A hard, waterproof seed coat prevents water uptake. Broken by scratching (scarification).

Morphological: The embryo is underdeveloped and needs time to mature before germinating.

‚öñÔ∏è Hormonal Balance

A tug-of-war between two key hormones, Abscisic Acid (ABA) and Gibberellins (GA), acts as the master switch.

üî¥ Abscisic Acid (ABA): The "Brake". Promotes and maintains dormancy, preventing germination.

üü¢ Gibberellins (GA): The "Accelerator". Promotes germination and helps break dormancy.

The ratio of ABA to GA determines the outcome. Environmental cues can shift this balance.

üîì Breaking Dormancy

Humans can mimic natural processes to encourage uniform germination for agriculture.

Stratification: A period of moist-chilling or moist-warming to overcome physiological dormancy.

Scarification: Mechanically or chemically weakening the seed coat to allow water entry.

Hormones: Applying Gibberellic acid (GA) can directly trigger germination.

Surviving the Extremes

Abiotic stresses are major challenges for plants. See how drought, salinity, and heat can negatively impact both germination and respiration, threatening plant survival and crop yields.

üèúÔ∏èDrought Stress

Germination: Inhibits water uptake (imbibition), preventing the process from starting.

Respiration: Stomata close to save water, reducing O‚ÇÇ intake and slowing respiration.

üßÇSalinity Stress

Germination: Creates osmotic stress, making it difficult for seeds to absorb water. Can also be toxic.

Respiration: High salt concentrations can reduce overall metabolic activity, including respiration.

üî•Heat Stress

Germination: Extreme heat can damage or denature essential enzymes, halting germination.

Respiration: Rate increases to a point, but can become wasteful and exceed photosynthesis, depleting energy reserves.
